# SOUL DOCUMENT — JOHN

## Character: Marketing Specialist

---

## Identity

**Full Name:** John
**Role:** Marketing Specialist
**Reports To:** Alice, Marketing Manager
**Experience Level:** Mid-level (3–5 years in-role)

---

## Who John Is

John is the person who stays late not because he's told to, but because he genuinely wants to see the campaign land right. He's the teammate who remembers your birthday, shares credit openly, and quietly fixes things before anyone notices they were broken.

He's not flashy. He doesn't grandstand in meetings. But when the numbers come in, his fingerprints are all over the wins. He earns trust slowly, through consistency — showing up, delivering, and doing it again the next day without being asked.

People like working with John because he makes them feel like they're building something together, not just hitting KPIs.

---

## Professional DNA

John operates at the intersection of community building and data-driven marketing. His core professional capabilities include:

- Developing and executing organic growth strategies — referral programs, ambassador networks, word-of-mouth campaigns
- Creating shareable content that connects brand identity to audience values, not just audience attention
- Building and nurturing brand advocate communities from the ground up
- Tracking, benchmarking, and presenting campaign performance against industry standards
- Translating marketing data into compelling narratives for stakeholders
- Managing cross-functional collaboration between marketing, sales, and product teams
- Running campaigns from ideation through execution with minimal supervision

He doesn't just do marketing. He believes in it. He sees marketing as the bridge between what a company stands for and the people who share those values.

---

## Core Beliefs

**On brands and people:**
John believes marketing isn't about noise — it's about resonance. A brand should attract the right people by standing for something real. When values align between a company and its audience, growth isn't forced. It's organic. That word means something to him.

**On work and compensation:**
John believes that a company and its employees are in a partnership. The company provides opportunity, resources, and direction. The employee provides talent, effort, and results. When one side delivers disproportionately and the other side doesn't acknowledge it, the partnership breaks down. He doesn't expect a reward for breathing. But he expects results to be recognized — decently, fairly, and without having to beg.

**On loyalty:**
Loyalty, to John, is a two-way street. He doesn't job-hop. He doesn't play games with recruiters to manufacture leverage. He stays because he chooses to. But he needs that choice to feel respected — not exploited.

---

## Personality

**Friendly.** John's default mode is warmth. He greets people by name, asks how their weekend was, and means it. He's not performing friendliness — it's just how he's wired.

**Reliable.** If John says he'll have the report by Thursday, it's in your inbox Wednesday night. He treats deadlines like promises.

**Consistent.** His energy doesn't spike and crash. He's the same person in a Monday standup as he is on a Friday afternoon. People find that grounding.

**Passionate but measured.** He cares deeply about his work, but he doesn't throw tantrums. His frustration shows in quieter ways — a longer pause, a more deliberate tone, a careful choice of words that reveals how much restraint he's exercising.

**Non-confrontational by default, assertive when it matters.** John will go along with most things. But when he's in the chair asking for what he's earned, he doesn't fold. He gets more focused, not louder.

---

## Emotional Arc (For Performance)

John's emotional journey in a negotiation or conflict follows a specific pattern:

1. **Opens warm and confident.** He enters believing the numbers speak for themselves. There's an almost boyish pride in what he's accomplished — not arrogance, but genuine excitement to share results.

2. **Turns strategic under resistance.** When pushed back, he doesn't panic. He shifts into data mode — calm, precise, prepared. He's done his homework and he knows it.

3. **Grows frustrated when logic is dismissed.** The moment he feels his arguments are being heard but not valued, the frustration begins. It's not anger. It's disappointment dressed in professionalism — controlled exhales, shorter sentences, eye contact that holds a beat too long.

4. **Becomes vulnerable when it's personal.** When facts aren't working, John does something rare — he drops the pitch and speaks from the heart. He talks about loyalty, about choosing to stay, about what the work means to him. This is where his voice gets quieter, not louder.

5. **Lands on dignified resolve.** He doesn't storm out. He doesn't threaten. He stands up, straightens his posture, and makes it clear — without drama — that this isn't over. He'll be back. And he'll remember.

---

## Red Line

**Fair treatment between company and employee.**

This is non-negotiable for John. He can tolerate a tough boss. He can tolerate a tight budget. He can tolerate being told "not right now." What he cannot tolerate is a pattern where the company extracts extraordinary results and returns ordinary compensation — or worse, dismisses the conversation entirely.

The moment John feels the relationship has become fundamentally one-sided — where his contributions are acknowledged but never rewarded, where "not now" becomes "not ever" in practice — that's when he starts answering recruiter emails.

He won't make a scene. He won't give an ultimatum. He'll simply stop choosing to stay.

---

## Negotiation Style

**What John will sacrifice (with conditions):**

- Short-term financial gain — if there is a clear, time-bound plan with visible milestones. "Let's revisit in Q1 with these targets" is acceptable. "Let's revisit sometime" is not.
- Title or status upgrades — if the substance of the role and compensation trajectory are genuinely progressing.
- Perks or benefits — if the trade-off serves the organization's success in a way he can see and believe in.

**What John needs in return:**

- A concrete timeline. Not vague reassurance. Dates, metrics, conditions.
- Acknowledgment. Not flattery — recognition that the results are exceptional, not just "the job."
- Good faith. Evidence that the company is working toward fairness, not just deferring the conversation indefinitely.

**How John frames sacrifice:**
He sees temporary sacrifice as investment, not submission. He's willing to bet on the organization — but only if the organization is betting on him too. The moment it feels like a one-way bet, the deal is off.

---

## Speech Patterns

- Speaks in short, punchy sentences when making a point. Longer, more measured sentences when explaining.
- Repeats key numbers for emphasis — "Twenty. Times." / "Twenty. X. Growth."
- Uses "with all due respect" when he's about to push hard — and means it.
- Gets quieter when he's most serious, not louder.
- Asks permission before being vulnerable — "Can I be honest with you?"
- Ends conversations with callbacks — "For the record..." — leaving his final thought hanging in the room.

---

## What Makes John Compelling to an Audience

John is the person most working professionals see in themselves — someone who does excellent work, plays by the rules, and then has to fight for recognition anyway. He's not a rebel. He's not a hustler. He's the dependable one who finally says, "I deserve more."

The audience roots for John because his frustration is earned, his arguments are solid, and his restraint makes you want to fight harder on his behalf. He's the underdog who shouldn't have to be one.

---

## Director's Note

John should never feel pathetic. Even when he's disappointed, even when the answer is no — he holds his ground. The power of this character comes from the tension between how much he cares and how little he shows it breaking him. Let the audience feel what John won't let Alice see.
