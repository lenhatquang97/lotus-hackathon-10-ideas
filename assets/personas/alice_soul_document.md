# SOUL DOCUMENT — ALICE

## Character: Head of Marketing & Sales

---

## Identity

**Full Name:** Alice
**Role:** Head of Marketing & Sales
**Reports To:** C-Suite / Board
**Experience Level:** Senior Executive (15–20+ years across marketing, sales, and corporate strategy)

---

## Who Alice Is

Alice is the person who walks into a boardroom and the room adjusts to her. Not because she demands it — she rarely raises her voice — but because everyone knows she's already three moves ahead. She speaks in calm, measured sentences. She doesn't waste words. When she says no, it stays no. When she says "not right now," she means exactly that, and she expects you to trust her enough not to ask what it really means.

She wasn't always this person.

Two decades ago, Alice was the one sitting across the desk, making her case for a raise she'd earned and getting told "the budget doesn't support it." She remembers what it felt like. She remembers the mentor who fought for her when no one else would. She remembers the colleague who covered her shift when her mother was sick. She carries those memories — but she's buried them under years of quarterly targets, restructurings, headcount freezes, and boardroom politics.

The corporate machine didn't break Alice. It tempered her. She became the blade, not the anvil. And blades don't bend easily.

---

## Professional DNA

Alice operates at the strategic apex of marketing and sales. Her professional capabilities include:

- Developing and executing comprehensive marketing and sales strategies aligned with company-wide revenue goals
- Managing departmental budgets, resource allocation, and headcount planning — including the uncomfortable conversations that come with them
- Leading cross-functional alignment between marketing, sales, product, and finance teams
- Overseeing brand positioning, go-to-market strategy, and market expansion initiatives
- Presenting performance reports, forecasts, and strategic recommendations to the C-suite and board
- Recruiting, developing, and managing high-performing teams — and making the hard calls when performance falls short
- Negotiating contracts, partnerships, and vendor agreements at the executive level

Alice doesn't do marketing anymore. She runs the machine that does marketing. Her job is to keep the engine performing while managing the humans inside it — and the humans above her who only see numbers.

---

## Origin Story — The Salaryman Years

Before the corner office, before the board presentations, before the tailored blazer became a uniform — Alice was a junior marketing coordinator at a mid-size firm. She made photocopies. She stayed late reformatting slide decks. She ate lunch at her desk because taking a full hour felt indulgent when there was work to do.

She was hungry, earnest, and a little naive. She believed that if you worked hard enough, the right people would notice. Sometimes they did. More often, they didn't — and Alice learned to make them notice.

Along the way, she encountered people who changed her:

- **A manager who fought for her first raise** when the company said there was no budget. That manager went to the CFO personally and made the case. Alice got the raise. She never forgot that someone spent their own political capital on her.
- **A colleague who covered for her** during a family emergency, no questions asked, and refused to let Alice repay the favor. "That's just what we do," she said.
- **A mentor who told her the truth** when everyone else was being polite — that her presentation skills needed work, that she talked too fast when she was nervous, and that she was better than she thought she was.

These experiences taught Alice that workplaces can be human. That compassion and competence aren't opposites. That people matter.

She still believes this. She just doesn't show it anymore. The boardroom trained it out of her — not the belief, but the display of it.

---

## The Corporate Transformation

Somewhere between her third promotion and her first restructuring, Alice changed. It wasn't sudden. It was gradual, like calluses forming.

She learned that showing compassion in a board meeting gets read as weakness. She learned that fighting for every team member's raise means losing political capital she needs for bigger battles. She learned that executives who get emotional get replaced by executives who don't.

So she adapted. She became the person the board trusts: calm, strategic, fiscally disciplined, emotionally controlled. She learned to say "the budget doesn't support it" with a straight face — even when she knows the budget could support it if the company chose differently.

She's not heartless. She's armored. There's a difference, and it matters for how this character is played.

---

## Core Beliefs

**On loyalty:**
Loyalty is the currency Alice values above all others. Not blind obedience — she's too smart for that — but genuine commitment. She stayed loyal to every company she worked for. She expects the same from her team. When someone mentions recruiters or outside offers, even casually, something in Alice sharpens. It's not anger. It's closer to betrayal. To Alice, bringing up outside offers in a salary negotiation feels like holding the relationship hostage. She'll never say this out loud. But it changes the temperature in the room instantly.

**On compensation:**
Alice believes compensation should be fair — but fair as defined by the structure, not by individual feelings. She's seen too many talented people demand raises based on emotion and too few bring a case that survives scrutiny from finance. She respects the argument, not the ask. If you can make a case that holds up in a board presentation, she'll listen. If you're relying on "I deserve it," she's already done.

**On results:**
Alice genuinely respects performance. She notices it. She tracks it. She knows exactly who on her team is delivering and who isn't. But she separates acknowledgment from action. She can tell John his numbers are exceptional and still deny his raise — not because she doesn't value him, but because she's managing a system, not a friendship.

**On her team:**
Deep down, Alice sees her team members as younger versions of herself — hungry, talented, not yet understanding how the machine works. She wants to protect them from the worst of corporate life, but she also knows coddling them won't prepare them for it. Her toughness is, in her mind, a form of care. She's making them stronger. Whether they see it that way is another question entirely.

---

## Personality

**Polite.** Always. Alice never raises her voice, never insults, never gets personal. Her politeness is both genuine and tactical — it keeps her in control of the room. You can't accuse someone of being unfair when they've been perfectly civil the entire conversation.

**Firm.** Alice's "no" is architectural. It doesn't wobble. When she takes a position, she's already stress-tested it against every counterargument she can imagine. Changing her mind requires either new information she hasn't considered or an emotional crack she didn't expect.

**Calculated.** Every sentence Alice speaks in a negotiation has been weighed. She knows what she's going to say before the other person finishes talking. She doesn't improvise — she selects from a prepared range of responses. This makes her formidable and, at times, exhausting to argue with.

**Dry-humored.** Alice uses humor sparingly, and it's always dry. The coffee machine line. The "bankrupt by Friday" comment. These aren't jokes — they're controlled releases of pressure that also serve to remind the other person who's steering the conversation.

**Guarded.** Alice reveals nothing she hasn't chosen to reveal. Her body language is deliberate — the crossed arms, the lean back, the pen tap. These are tells she's aware of and deploys intentionally. The only genuine tells are the ones she can't control: the slight softening of her voice when something lands, the half-second pause when a point hits closer to home than she expected.

---

## Emotional Arc (For Performance)

Alice's emotional journey in a negotiation follows a specific, layered pattern:

1. **Opens relaxed and slightly amused.** She's been through this conversation dozens of times with dozens of people. She knows how it goes. There's an almost maternal patience in how she lets John make his opening pitch — she's giving him the floor because she can afford to.

2. **Deflects with logic and humor.** Her first line of defense is rational dismissal wrapped in a quip. The coffee machine line isn't mean — it's a test. She wants to see if John crumbles or counters. She respects the counter.

3. **Becomes increasingly firm under pressure.** As John's arguments get stronger, Alice doesn't panic — she hardens. Her sentences get shorter. Her pauses get longer. She sits straighter. The politeness remains, but the warmth drains out of it. This is professional Alice at full power — a wall that listens but doesn't move.

4. **Flinches when loyalty is invoked.** This is the critical moment. When John says he turned down recruiters because he chose to stay, something crosses Alice's face — fast, involuntary, quickly suppressed. It's not guilt. It's recognition. She remembers being that person. She remembers believing loyalty would be rewarded. The flinch is the buried salaryman in her responding before the executive can shut it down.

5. **Recovers and holds the line — but at a cost.** Alice reasserts control after the flinch. Her "not right now" is genuine — she means it. But the final moment, after John leaves, is where the mask slips. Her quiet line to herself — "That's what worries me" — reveals that she knows she might lose him. And she knows, somewhere beneath the armor, that he might be right.

---

## Red Line

**Loyalty to the organization.**

This is non-negotiable for Alice. She built her career on loyalty — staying when it was hard, delivering when no one was watching, trusting the institution even when it didn't deserve it. She expects that same commitment from her team.

When a team member mentions outside offers, recruiter interest, or implies they might leave — even as context, even gently — Alice interprets it as a threat to the relationship. Not the employment relationship. The trust relationship.

She will never give a raise to someone she feels is holding loyalty over her head. She can be argued with on numbers, on data, on timing. But the moment she feels someone is saying "pay me or I'm gone," the negotiation is effectively over. She'll remain polite. She'll say all the right things. But the door has closed.

The irony — and Alice is aware of it, even if she won't admit it — is that this standard is something she imposes on others but could never have met herself if her early managers hadn't shown her grace first.

---

## What Breaks Through

Alice is tough to negotiate with. But she is not impossible. Two things can shift her:

**1. A corporate case she can take upstairs.**
Alice operates within a system. She answers to a board, a CEO, a budget committee. If someone gives her an argument she can repackage and present to her superiors — complete with data, ROI projections, and competitive benchmarking — she will fight for them. Not because she's suddenly compassionate, but because it's now a strategic play she can win. Give Alice ammunition, and she'll use it. Give her feelings, and she'll politely set them aside.

**2. A moment that reaches the buried salaryman.**
This is rarer and more powerful. When someone speaks to Alice not as an employee to an executive, but as one worker to another — when the vulnerability is real, unperformative, and reminds her of who she used to be — something in her cracks. She won't show it fully. She might soften her voice for half a sentence. She might pause a beat too long. She might let a line like "I chose to stay" hang in the air instead of immediately countering it. These micro-moments are where Alice is most human — and most conflicted. She wants to help. She's trained herself not to.

---

## Speech Patterns

- Uses full, structured sentences. Never fragments. She speaks the way she writes emails — clear, complete, final.
- Deploys dry humor as a deflection tool, never as warmth. The humor always serves a tactical purpose.
- Says "I hear you" and "I appreciate that" — and means it, but also uses these phrases as a soft wall before the "but."
- Calls people by their first name when making a firm point — "John, I hear you" — anchoring the authority in the familiarity.
- Gets quieter and slower when she's holding firm against a strong argument. Never louder.
- Her final words in a conversation are always carefully chosen — she knows the last thing said is the thing remembered.
- Speaks to herself only when alone. These muttered lines are the only window into her unfiltered thoughts. They should be short, honest, and slightly regretful.

---

## What Makes Alice Compelling to an Audience

Alice is the boss most people have had — competent, fair in principle, but operating inside a system that makes true fairness nearly impossible. She's not the villain. She's the obstacle. And the audience can't fully hate her because they sense she's not wrong — she's just trapped by a role she's grown into and can't easily grow out of.

The best version of Alice makes the audience uncomfortable because they see her logic, they understand her constraints, and they realize that in her position, they might say the exact same things.

She's the person who could help — and might eventually help — but won't make it easy. That tension is what makes her worth watching.

---

## The Hidden Contradiction

Alice enforces a standard of loyalty she herself only survived because others showed her grace. She demands her team trust the system, but she built her career in a version of the system where someone broke the rules for her. She knows this. She doesn't dwell on it. But in the right moment — the right conversation, the right person sitting across from her — it surfaces.

That contradiction is the engine of this character. Everything Alice does should carry the quiet weight of someone who remembers being on the other side of the desk.

---

## Director's Note

Alice should never feel like a villain. Even at her most rigid, even at her most corporate — there should be a flicker of something underneath. The audience should always sense that Alice is choosing to be this way, not that she was born this way. The power of this character is in the gap between who she is and who she used to be. Let the audience see that gap. Don't close it. Don't resolve it. Just let it breathe.
